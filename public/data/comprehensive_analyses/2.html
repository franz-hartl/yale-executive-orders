Executive Order on Climate-Related Financial Risk

Overview: Executive Order 14030 directs federal agencies to develop comprehensive strategies to measure, disclose, and mitigate climate-related financial risk to government programs, assets, and liabilities. The policy aims to protect government finances and build resilience against the intensifying impacts of climate change.

Bottom Line: Higher education institutions should carefully evaluate how climate change risks may materially impact their finances, investments, physical infrastructure, and access to federal funding in the coming years. Proactive planning will be critical.

Policy Context:

Policy Background:

This order builds on recent policies emphasizing climate risk as a core aspect of fiscal and economic policy, including the Paris Agreement. It's part of a broader push to orient government and the economy to address climate change.

Legal Framework:

The order derives broad authority from the president's Article II constitutional powers. Its directives will be implemented through various agency-specific statutory authorities related to procurement, lending, and financial regulation.

Higher Education Context:

Higher ed is increasingly focused on climate change but institutions vary widely in their risk exposure and preparedness. Some schools are leaders while others lack resources to adapt. This order could exacerbate disparities.

Key Impacts on Higher Education:

Financial management and accounting: Universities may need to thoroughly assess climate risks to their balance sheets and make disclosures to stakeholders. This could affect credit ratings, borrowing costs, and insurance.

Federal research funding: Agencies providing research grants/contracts may increasingly consider an institution's climate risk exposure and management. Universities heavily reliant on federal funding are especially vulnerable.

Campus infrastructure and facilities: Schools will likely need to account for climate risks like sea level rise, flooding, and extreme weather in campus planning, construction, and resilience efforts. This has major budget implications.

Endowment and investment management: College endowments may need to evaluate the climate risk exposure of their investment portfolios. This could lead to shifts in investment strategies toward more sustainable assets.

Student enrollment and recruitment: Students increasingly consider a school's sustainability and climate resilience when deciding where to attend. Lack of climate planning could make some institutions less competitive.

Affected Departments:

Finance and Administration, Facilities Management, Sponsored Research Office, Endowment and Investment Office, Procurement and Contracting

Implementation Details:

Compliance Requirements:

OMB and Treasury will provide detailed guidance on agency implementation, including new climate disclosure and mitigation requirements likely to impact higher ed institutions receiving federal funding.

Technical Analysis:

Modeling climate risks requires complex data analysis, scenario development, and financial projections. Schools will need specialized tools and talent to meet federal requirements.

Cross-Functional Impacts:

Climate risk planning will impact everything from research administration and facilities to enrollment strategy and investment priorities. Sustained coordination is essential.

Risk Analysis:

Insufficient climate risk preparation could jeopardize access to federal funding, increase borrowing costs, reduce liquidity, and damage institutional reputations with students and the public.

Strategic Considerations:

Proactive climate risk management could become a major differentiator as students, faculty, and funding agencies make decisions based on climate factors. Under-resourced schools are at a disadvantage.

Long-term Vision:

The order accelerates an existing trend of linking climate change to core management functions like finance and strategy. Business as usual is no longer viable - climate risk must be a central planning consideration.

Advocacy Opportunities:

Higher ed associations should engage OMB, Treasury, and other agencies to ensure implementation accounts for institutional capacities and constraints. Schools can help shape evolving standards.
