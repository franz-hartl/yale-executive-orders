Executive Order 2025-03138: Reducing Higher Education Regulatory Burden

Overview: This executive order is part of a broader administration initiative to improve government efficiency through deregulation. It specifically directs federal agencies to identify regulations affecting higher education institutions that can be repealed or modified to reduce compliance burdens. The stated goal is to lower administrative costs for universities while maintaining essential oversight.

Bottom Line: Higher education institutions should proactively assess their regulatory compliance posture, engage with policymakers during the review process, and prepare to adapt to a changing regulatory landscape.

Policy Context:

Policy Background:

Deregulation has been a priority for multiple administrations, with a focus on reducing perceived government overreach and lowering compliance costs for businesses and other entities. This order extends that focus to higher education, an area with substantial federal involvement.

Legal Framework:

The order relies on the statutory authority of the president to direct federal agencies in their execution of law. Specific changes to regulations must still go through the normal rulemaking process. Stakeholders will have opportunities to provide input.

Higher Education Context:

Higher education has seen increasing regulatory requirements over time, often in areas like student aid, research compliance, and reporting. Institutions have long advocated for a more streamlined approach. However, some argue that strong oversight is necessary to protect students and ensure accountability.

Key Impacts on Higher Education:

Regulatory Compliance Costs: Universities may see reduced costs associated with compliance activities if substantial deregulation occurs. However, the extent of savings depends on the specific regulations changed and each institution's existing compliance infrastructure.

Administrative Workload: Changes to regulations could decrease certain administrative burdens, such as reporting requirements, freeing up staff time. However, understanding and adapting to new regulatory parameters may add work in the short term.

Institutional Risk Management: Shifts in the regulatory landscape will require universities to re-examine their risk exposure and adjust mitigation strategies. Less regulation may mean more flexibility but could also increase uncertainty.

Research and Sponsored Programs: If deregulation affects research compliance requirements (e.g. human subjects protection, animal welfare, export controls), it could impact university research programs and sponsored projects.

Student Financial Aid: Changes to regulations around federal student aid programs would have significant impacts on students and enrollment management. Universities will need to adapt policies and processes accordingly.

Until specific regulations are officially changed, universities must maintain compliance with all current requirements. Failure to do so could result in federal penalties. Institutions should document a good faith effort to track and adapt to the evolving regulatory landscape.

Technical Analysis:

Certain regulations, such as those dealing with grants management systems, data security, or financial aid processing, may have significant technical implications. IT, research, and financial aid systems may require updates to reflect new standards. Technical experts should be engaged early.

Cross-Functional Impacts:

Regulatory changes in one area (e.g. research) are likely to have spillover effects in other domains (e.g. legal, financial, IT). A holistic approach to assessing and responding to new requirements will be essential. Leadership should empower cross-functional working groups.

Risk Analysis:

Deregulation may change the type and magnitude of compliance risks universities face. Less prescriptive oversight could increase the onus on institutions to define and enforce appropriate standards. Penalties for non-compliance may also shift. Risk managers must adapt their frameworks.

Deregulation could have differential impacts based on institution type. Larger universities may have more resources to adapt to changes and take advantage of new flexibility. Smaller institutions may struggle more to adjust. This could affect competitive dynamics in some areas.

Universities should actively engage in the regulatory review and rulemaking processes, both individually and through industry associations. Effective advocacy can help shape the direction of reform. Campus government affairs offices will play a central role.
